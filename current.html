Darren Fayne
Head of Research Group
Background
Darren holds an honours degree in Applied Chemistry and PhD in computational and organic chemistry from Dublin City University. He has an M.Ed. in Higher Education from Trinity College Dublin. He worked for two years at Solvay Pharmaceuticals in Hannover, Germany as a molecular designer from March 2003. While with the Computer-Aided Drug Design Group he focused on two main therapeutic areas of research: cardiovascular disease and gastroenterology (IBS/IBD). In July 2005 Darren joined the Molecular Design Group in Trinity College Dublin as a Senior Research Fellow. In January 2024 Darren started a permanent position as an Assistant Professor (above bar) in Computational Chemistry in DCU.
Research Projects
Darren has lead a number of successful small molecule hit-finding research programmes against various protein targets including – androgen receptor (AR), farnesoid X receptor (FXR), peroxisome proliferating activated receptor gamma (PPARγ), glucocorticoid receptor (GR), estrogen receptor α and β (ER), tubulin, caspase 6, macrophage migration inhibitory factor (MIF), toll-like receptor 4/myeloid differentiation 2 (TLR4/MD2), plasmepsins, IL-17, Viral infectivity factor (Vif), Lipoprotein signal peptidase (LspA), cytochrome P450, SARS-CoV-2 nsp3, SARS- CoV-2 nsp13, along with novel compounds demonstrating in vivo efficacy against malignant pleural mesothelioma, lung cancer and sepsis. In the late 2000’s, he co- founded and served as a director of a rational drug design campus spin-out company to commercialise intellectual property and develop compounds for the treatment of an orphan cancer – malignant pleural mesothelioma.

Ivan Čmelo
Postdoctoral Researcher
Background
Ivan holds a Master's degree in Informatics and Chemistry from the University of Chemistry and Technology Prague (UCT Prague), and a PhD in cheminformatics from the same university. His doctoral work focused on the computational exploration of chemical space, including the development of algorithms for structure generation. His work also extended into virtual screening and synthetic accessibility estimation. Ivan has held research positions at the Czech Institute of Molecular Genetics and at the Department of Informatics and Chemistry at UCT Prague, where he has contributed to cheminformatics, forensic/data analysis and IT infrastructure development. He has developed and deployed cheminformatic software used in drug discovery, data analysis and forensics. In 2022, he was awarded a GoIPD Fellowship hosted at Trinity College Dublin in collaboration with prof. Darren Fayne, with whom he later transferred to Dublin City University as part of the Molecule Design Group.
Research Projects
Ivan has contributed to multiple research projects spanning cheminformatics, computational drug design and chemical forensics. For his PhD, he created and screened a million-compound virtual library of potential steroid receptor ligands. He also developed the Feature Interrelation Profiling methodology for estimating compound properties based on mutual information and Kullback-Leibler divergence patterns between compound structural features in different datasets. His current postdoc research pertains to target fishing, i.e. identifying novel biological targets for given molecules using an ensemble of machine learning models. In parallel, Ivan has maintained active collaborations with synthetic and forensic chemists and developed new collaborations with international groups specializing in drug discovery and in novel machine learning methodologies. His research outputs constitute open-source cheminformatics libraries and tools, journal publications, monitoring programmes like PharmaCrime and outreach community programmes such as DrugsInfoSite.

Shubhangi Kandwal
4th-Year PhD Candidate in Biochemistry
Background
Shubhangi has completed bachelor’s degree in pharmacy from Delhi Pharmaceutical Sciences and Research University, New Delhi, India, in 2019. She also graduated with M.Sc. degree in Molecular Medicine from Trinity College Dublin, Ireland, in 2020. During the master’s degree, her thesis project, “Repurposing drugs for treatment of SARS-CoV-2 infection: computational design insights into mechanisms of action,” was published as a research article in the Journal of Biomolecular Structure and Dynamics. In the year 2021, she was awarded the Government of Ireland Postgraduate Award by the Irish Research Council to pursue her PhD studies.
Research Project
Every virus present in nature has emerged from a common ancestral family. For example, viruses such as Zika, chikungunya, coronaviruses, the dengue virus, hepatitis viruses and more belong to a family of viruses called the Group-4 viruses. In my project, I have identified common features, i.e., common binding sites, that are present in related proteins among these viruses. The idea is to find a chemical entity, a potential "drug", that will bind to these similar binding sites. We have identified common binding sites present in the helicase (nsp13) and Mac1 proteins (nsp3) of the SARS-CoV-2 virus and other Group-4 viruses. Since we have already identified bioactive hit compounds for the Mac1 domain of nsp3, we envisage that these might also bind to the Mac1 domain of few of the Group-4 viruses. The idea is to identify a common key (drug) for a common lock (protein).

Chao Chen
Application Developer
Background
Chao obtained his bachelor's degree in a computer science-related field in China in 2009 and subsequently worked as a software engineer. Chao is currently employed as an application developer at Fiserv. In 2022, Chao began a part-time master's program at Dublin City University (DCU), and in 2024, he commenced his final project under the supervision of Assistant Professor Darren.
Research Project
Chao's research focuses on distributed graph algorithms, which are based on the principle of "thinking like a vertex." Currently, Chao is developing a distributed application for pharmacophore mapping, matching, and virtual screening. Since molecules and pharmacophores can be naturally modeled as graphs, applying distributed graph algorithms enables faster and more scalable virtual screening.
Personal Interests
Chao is particularly interested in application of distributed graph processing, knowledge graphs, and graph neural networks.

Oisín Keating Mac Dermott
3rd Year INTRA Student
Background
Oisín is a 3 rd Year Chemistry and Artificial Intelligence from Dublin. He is completing a research project with the Group as part of his course placement. Oisín has a background in emergency medical service and bartending.
Research Project
Oisín  researched under Dr Tilo Grosser and Dr Liz Hennessey in the Institute of Translational Medicine and Therapeutics at the University of Pennsylvania in 2019. Here he investigated the effects of the COX-2 knockout gene on the phenotype of M1 pro- inflammatory macrophages. He examined the morphological changes to the macrophage cell membranes, using a cell culturing method that involved the extraction of mouse femur bone marrow, and incubation with macrophage colony stimulation factor. Using analytical techniques such as liquid chromatography and mass spectrometry, he then processed the supernatants and identified the molecular mass of the prostaglandins with indicators attached to them. Finally he verified the lipopolysaccharide response in all cell cultures, and analysed the results. Oisín is currently developing two machine learning models to predict Distribution and Toxicity properties of small molecules as part of his work with the Molecular Design Group.
